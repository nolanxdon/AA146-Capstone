\begin{abstract}
This paper documents the present end-to-end state of a blown-wing design workflow developed for a \SI{5}{kg}-class distributed-propulsion aircraft. The project begins with a fast Stage~1 Pareto-front optimizer that screens propeller count, propeller diameter, pitch ratio, and coarse propeller family while solving operating RPM internally. Stage~1 does \emph{not} collapse its objectives into a weighted scalar score; instead, feasible architectures are filtered by true three-objective nondominance in low-speed electrical power, loiter energy, and propulsion mass. The resulting shortlist is then carried into a fixed-span geometry refinement, a multi-airfoil front-end section study, a DAE51-centered rectangular-wing control-surface sizing study, a motor-height sensitivity study, and a frozen-geometry speed sweep from \SIrange{5}{15}{m/s} with RPM re-solved at each speed. The work is motivated primarily by two recent blown-wing studies. Hawkswell et al. demonstrated that a low-order, uniform two-dimensional jet model captures the first-order behaviour of a blown wing so long as the wing remains immersed in the jet, and further showed that propeller position and jet size govern the onset of abrupt stall.\cite{Hawkswell2022} Agrawal et al. experimentally characterized a blown slotted-flap wing in terms of section $c_l$, $c_x$, and $c_m$ as functions of angle of attack, flap angle, motor-axis angle, and jet momentum-excess coefficient, and showed that aggressive blown high-lift systems can achieve very large section lift while also incurring strong pitching-moment and stall-shape penalties.\cite{Agrawal2019} These two papers motivate the present modeling split: the blown portion of the wing is treated as a jet-covered two-dimensional strip, but aircraft-level $C_L$ and $C_M$ are always assembled by area-weighting blown and unblown portions of the wing at common $\alpha$. The current Stage~1 sweep evaluates 1386 propulsion architectures, of which 34 satisfy the present feasibility constraints and 11 survive onto the Pareto front. The selected study concept, referred to throughout as rank~6, is a $10\times 5.5\times 2.2$~in balanced propeller arrangement that was retained manually from that nondominated set because it sits near the low-cruise-power edge while also preserving the highest blown-wing momentum coefficient in the screened family. The downstream DAE51 control-surface study indicates that the present concept supports a large, overlap-aware inboard single-slotted flap and a viable outboard aileron, but still falls short of the \SI{4.0}{m/s} target low-speed condition once the common-$\alpha$ whole-wing lift curve is used. The motor-height trade produces the expected three-part trend: lift first improves as immersion is recovered, then saturates, then degrades as the motor is dropped too far and the modeled stall becomes more abrupt. The new off-design speed sweep further shows that the clean branch carries most of the feasible corridor, while the deployed-flap branches become increasingly drag- and thrust-limited away from the low-speed design point. The full repository structure, equations, plots, and findings are recorded here in implementation-matched form so that future work can replace the current surrogates with experimentally calibrated or CFD-calibrated relationships without losing the logic of the present design space exploration.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The present project addresses a classic blown-wing design problem under unusually tight geometric constraints. The aircraft span is fixed at \SI{2.0}{m}, the low-speed operating point is set at \SI{4.0}{m/s}, the cruise operating point is set at \SI{10.0}{m/s}, and the gross design mass target is \SI{5.0}{kg}. These conditions immediately imply a demanding freestream-referenced lift requirement. Using the rectangular baseline wing of span \(b=\SI{2.0}{m}\) and chord \(c=\SI{0.35}{m}\), the freestream dynamic pressure at \SI{4}{m/s} is only about \SI{9.8}{Pa}; the corresponding freestream-referenced aircraft lift requirement is
\begin{equation}
C_{L,\mathrm{req}} = \frac{W}{q_\infty S} \approx 7.15,
\end{equation}
which is far beyond conventional unblown wing capability. The design task is therefore not to make a mildly assisted wing, but to identify a propulsion architecture and high-lift system that can make a very-low-speed blown wing credible without producing unreasonable cruise penalties.

The workflow implemented in this repository was built to answer five questions in sequence:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Which propeller architectures are energetically attractive once geometric packing, thrust, and blown-lift constraints are enforced?
\item Which architecture should be carried forward into more detailed wing and control-surface work?
\item What flap, aileron, and motor-height choices are consistent with a blown slotted-flap concept once the wing is treated as a mixture of blown and unblown regions?
\item What operating-point implications follow for motor selection, trim burden, and low-speed controllability?
\item How does the frozen wing and motor-height configuration behave off-design once RPM is re-solved from \SIrange{5}{15}{m/s}?
\end{enumerate}

The present repository therefore combines a coarse Pareto-front architecture screener with more detailed follow-on studies. The philosophy is deliberately hierarchical: use a simple but broad Stage~1 optimizer to downselect the propeller family, then impose literature-backed blown-wing physics on a smaller set of architectures. This is the same general spirit seen in Hawkswell et al., who used low-order reasoning to explore the coupled role of propeller size, position, and wing immersion in the jet before optimizing more specific aircraft-level outcomes,\cite{Hawkswell2022} and in Agrawal et al., who emphasized fitting section response surfaces from test data rather than pretending that one single momentum-theory equation captures every aspect of a blown flap.\cite{Agrawal2019}

\section{Literature Basis and Modeling Philosophy}

\subsection{Hawkswell et al.: jet immersion, finite jet size, and propeller placement}

Hawkswell et al. are the primary motivation for the present motor-height and blown-strip modeling strategy.\cite{Hawkswell2022} Three findings from that paper are central.

First, they show that the wing must remain within the propeller jet across the relevant operating range. Their wording is unusually direct: if the wing moves outside the jet, even slightly, the wing can stall suddenly.\cite{Hawkswell2022} That insight motivates the present repository's decision to model motor height primarily through a jet-immersion criterion rather than through an arbitrary empirical bonus factor.

Second, Hawkswell et al. demonstrate that a uniform two-dimensional actuator-disc treatment is an acceptable first-order approximation of the blown-wing problem so long as the main concern is first-order lift behaviour. They compared two-dimensional and three-dimensional actuator-disc variants and concluded that, provided the wing remains submerged in the jet, variations in jet height have only second-order effect on the overall lift distribution and peak lift.\cite{Hawkswell2022} This finding is why the present repository is comfortable treating the blown strip as a nominally uniform two-dimensional region even though the final wing is finite.

Third, Hawkswell et al. show that there is an important balance between jet size and jet velocity. A smaller propeller for fixed thrust increases jet velocity, which can steepen the lift response with flap angle; however, the smaller jet height also makes it easier for the jet to miss the wing, reducing the stall margin.\cite{Hawkswell2022} This balance is one of the main reasons Stage~1 does not simply minimize power or maximize jet velocity in isolation. The selected architecture must balance cruise power, induced blowing authority, and geometric coverage of the wing.

The same paper also provides practical motor-height scales that are used in the repository as anchors, not as exact fitted constants. Their idealized no-nacelle configuration found a favorable position near \(0.21c\) below the wing centerline, while the more practical nacelle-included analysis brought the favorable scale closer to \(0.12c\).\cite{Hawkswell2022} In the present code these values are treated as the main \emph{paper-derived} motor-height scales, while the precise surrogate coefficients around them remain \emph{paper-informed} or \emph{heuristic}.

\subsection{Agrawal et al.: section response, slotted flaps, pitching moment, and aggressive stall}

Agrawal et al. provide the strongest direct experimental foundation for the present flap and wing-only section-response interpretation.\cite{Agrawal2019} Their wind-tunnel model was instrumented to vary flap angle, motor-axis angle, blowing level, and motor height, and they reported response surfaces in terms of section \(c_l\), \(c_x\), and \(c_m\). That format is particularly important for this project because it clarifies that large blown-wing lift is fundamentally a \emph{section plus jet} effect before it is an aircraft-level \(C_L\) effect.

Two observations from Agrawal et al. directly influenced the current code.

The first is their experimental magnitude: section \(c_l\) values near 8--9 were observed at strong blowing levels and aggressive flap deflections.\cite{Agrawal2019} This motivated the current repository change from overinflated freestream-scaled section curves to capped, common-\(\alpha\) section curves whose magnitudes remain within the same order as the experimental paper.

The second is their discussion of pitch and stall behaviour. Agrawal et al. note that increasing blowing changes \(c_m\), and that the sign and magnitude of this change depend strongly on flap angle and angle of attack.\cite{Agrawal2019} They further note that the \(20^\circ\) motor-axis-angle case appears to stall more suddenly and more aggressively than the \(10^\circ\) case in the \(40^\circ\) flap configuration, which they interpret as earlier upper-surface separation because the jet must bend through a larger angle to remain attached.\cite{Agrawal2019} That insight directly motivates the present repository's \emph{over-drop stall penalty}: a second mechanism, separate from immersion, that is intended to capture the idea that an overly aggressive motor/jet geometry can sharpen stall even after the wing is fully immersed in the jet.

\subsection{What is paper-derived, paper-informed, and heuristic?}

The distinction between evidence levels matters in this project.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Paper-derived}: the most direct anchors are the need for jet immersion, the practical motor-height scale near \(0.12c\), the idealized motor-height scale near \(0.21c\), the validity of a uniform two-dimensional jet-covered strip as a first-order approximation, and the order-of-magnitude section lift values reported by Agrawal et al.\cite{Hawkswell2022,Agrawal2019}
\item \textbf{Paper-informed}: the existence of separate clean and flap branches in the motor-height trade, the use of an over-drop penalty that activates mainly near stall, and the interpretation that flap-aligned propeller coverage matters because the jet/flap interaction is central to the lift benefit.\cite{Hawkswell2022,Agrawal2019}
\item \textbf{Heuristic / to be calibrated}: the exact steepness of the immersion switch, the onset values for the over-drop penalty, the polynomial weighting of low-angle and near-stall slotted-flap gains, and the motor-targeting safety factors.
\end{itemize}

The present paper keeps this distinction explicit because the repository is intended as a design workflow, not as a claim that every coefficient has already been fully calibrated.

This script architecture is not incidental; it embodies the philosophy of the project. Stage~1 is intentionally broad and cheap. The later files are narrower and more physically specific, but they only run after the architecture space has been pruned. This is the computational analogue of the design logic argued by Hawkswell et al.: use low-order reasoning to understand the main architectural trade before investing in more expensive analysis.\cite{Hawkswell2022}

\subsection{Methodical data flow among scripts}

The repository now behaves as a sequential research workflow rather than a loose collection of notebooks and post-processing utilities. In practical terms, the scripts communicate through explicit artifact handoffs:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \texttt{stage1\_screen.py} generates the full architecture sweep and the nondominated shortlist in CSV form.
\item \texttt{stage2\_prop\_span.py} reconstructs explicit propeller centers, packing margins, and blown-span fractions for the shortlisted architectures.
\item \texttt{airfoil\_polar\_comparison.py} generates the front-end section database and paper-style airfoil response grids.
\item \texttt{control\_surface\_sizing.py} reads a selected architecture and airfoil, sizes the rectangular-wing flap and aileron, and writes the common-\(\alpha\) whole-wing \(C_L\) and \(C_M\) curves.
\item \texttt{motor\_height\_trade.py} freezes that geometry and sweeps only motor drop, separating the clean-blown and slotted-flap-blown branches.
\item \texttt{wing\_speed\_sweep.py} then freezes airfoil, control geometry, and motor height, re-solves RPM at each speed and flap state, and reports the off-design operating-point behavior.
\item \texttt{validate\_outputs.py} is finally used to ensure that CSV schemas, row counts, and expected plot artifacts remain synchronized after each code change.
\end{enumerate}
This handoff logic matters because it keeps the same concept definition --- propeller count, diameter, pitch ratio, airfoil, flap geometry, aileron geometry, and motor-drop assumption --- flowing through the downstream studies without silent changes in interpretation.

\subsection{Propeller and blowing surrogate}

The Stage~1 propulsion model remains a surrogate, but it is internally consistent. The current thrust and shaft power relations are
\begin{align}
T_{\mathrm{per}} &= C_T \rho n^2 D_p^4, \\
P_{\mathrm{shaft,per}} &= C_P \rho n^3 D_p^5,
\end{align}
where \(n=\mathrm{RPM}/60\) and \(C_T\) and \(C_P\) are taken from coarse propeller-family fits. The purpose of these fits is not to identify a final propeller, but to keep the Stage~1 optimizer broad enough to search the architecture space quickly.

The propeller coefficients are themselves functions of advance ratio and blade Reynolds number. The code first defines
\begin{equation}
J = \frac{V}{n D_p},
\end{equation}
then applies a family-dependent static scaling
\begin{align}
C_{T,\mathrm{static}} &= C_{T,\mathrm{base}}
\left(\frac{P/D}{(P/D)_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right)^{0.35}, \\
C_{P,\mathrm{static}} &= C_{P,\mathrm{base}}
\left(\frac{P/D}{(P/D)_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right)^{0.80},
\end{align}
followed by a thrust and power roll-off with advance ratio:
\begin{align}
f_T(J) &= \max\!\left(0,\; 1 - \left(\frac{J}{J_{\mathrm{crit}}}\right)^{1.45}\right), \\
f_P(J) &= \max\!\left(0.10,\; 1 - \left(\frac{J}{1.15J_{\mathrm{crit}}}\right)^{1.60}\right).
\end{align}
Finally, a blade Reynolds penalty is applied:
\begin{equation}
f_{Re} = \mathrm{clip}\!\left[\left(\frac{Re_b}{Re_{\mathrm{ref}}}\right)^{m_{Re}},\;
f_{Re,\min},\; 1\right].
\end{equation}
The resulting implementation-matched coefficients are
\begin{align}
C_T &= C_{T,\mathrm{static}} f_T(J) f_{Re}, \\
C_P &= C_{P,\mathrm{static}} \left(0.25 + 0.75 f_P(J)\right) f_{Re}.
\end{align}
The corresponding total electrical power and per-propeller torque are then
\begin{align}
P_{\mathrm{elec,tot}} &= \frac{P_{\mathrm{shaft,tot}}}{\eta_{\mathrm{elec}}} + P_{\mathrm{avionics}}, \\
Q_{\mathrm{per}} &= \frac{P_{\mathrm{shaft,per}}}{2\pi n}.
\end{align}
These equations are used in the code to convert each candidate's geometry and solved RPM into total thrust, shaft power, electrical power, torque, and tip Mach. They are the main bridge between the architecture variables \((N_p,D_p,P/D,\text{family})\) and the low-speed/high-speed feasibility checks.

The total effective blown velocity is then obtained from an actuator-disc interpretation:
\begin{align}
v_i &= \sqrt{\frac{T_{\mathrm{tot}}}{2\rho A_{\mathrm{disk}}}}, \\
V_{\mathrm{eff}} &= V_\infty + 2v_i.
\end{align}
Stage~1 also defines a wing-reference momentum coefficient,
\begin{equation}
C_\mu = \frac{T_{\mathrm{tot}}}{q_\infty S},
\end{equation}
which is later used as one measure of blown authority.

This is the main Stage~1 blown-wing objective metric and is the repository analogue of the low-order blown-wing forcing parameter emphasized by Hawkswell et al.\cite{Hawkswell2022} The airfoil-front-end study also keeps Agrawal et al.'s jet momentum-excess notation in view. Their section experiments are parameterized by
\begin{equation}
\Delta c_J = 2c_Q\left(\frac{V_J}{V_\infty} - \frac{V_\infty}{V_J}\right),
\end{equation}
which is not identical to the repository's \(C_\mu\), but serves the same conceptual purpose of measuring the strength of the jet relative to the freestream.\cite{Agrawal2019} The present code therefore uses \(C_\mu\) in Stage~1 and representative \(\Delta c_J\)-style section plots in the airfoil front-end without pretending they are identical quantities.

The choice to keep both \(V_{\mathrm{eff}}\) and \(C_\mu\) in the optimizer is directly motivated by Hawkswell et al. Their discussion of jet size versus jet velocity implies that one cannot rank architectures purely by thrust, purely by power, or purely by jet speed: the candidate must also preserve enough jet coverage to keep the wing immersed.\cite{Hawkswell2022}

\subsection{Area-weighted blown-wing feasibility}

The main low-speed feasibility question in Stage~1 is not whether the unblown wing can fly at \SI{4}{m/s}; it clearly cannot. The question is whether the propeller architecture can create enough blown-strip dynamic pressure and blown-section capability to keep the working local \(c_l\) below a conservative ceiling.

The baseline Stage~1 low-speed model therefore divides the wing into unblown and blown regions and evaluates the required blown velocity from the remaining lift after the unblown region has done all it can. In simplified form,
\begin{equation}
L_{\mathrm{rem}} = W - q_\infty S_u c_{l,\mathrm{ceil}},
\end{equation}
and the required blown-strip dynamic pressure follows as
\begin{equation}
q_{b,\mathrm{req}} = \frac{L_{\mathrm{rem}}}{S_b c_{l,\mathrm{ceil}}}.
\end{equation}
The corresponding required effective blown velocity is
\begin{equation}
V_{\mathrm{eff,req}} = \sqrt{\frac{2q_{b,\mathrm{req}}}{\rho}}.
\end{equation}

This is not yet a full Agrawal-style \(\Delta c_J\) surface fit, but it serves the same design purpose: it forces the optimizer to ask how much local blown dynamic pressure is actually needed to carry the wing at the low-speed operating point, rather than presuming the answer in advance.\cite{Agrawal2019}

The Stage~1 low-speed drag estimate is then assembled from separate blown and unblown strips:
\begin{align}
k &= \frac{1}{\pi e AR}, \\
C_{D,u} &= \gamma_{\mathrm{trim}}
\left(f_{u} C_{D0,\mathrm{flap}} + k c_{l,u}^{2}\right), \\
C_{D,b} &= \gamma_{\mathrm{trim}}
\left(f_{b} C_{D0,\mathrm{flap}} + k c_{l,b}^{2}\right),
\end{align}
and
\begin{equation}
D_{\mathrm{low}} = q_\infty S_u C_{D,u} + q_b S_b C_{D,b}.
\end{equation}
The role of this equation in the solver is straightforward: low-speed thrust must exceed both the drag-driven requirement and the blowing-driven requirement, so the propeller system is only considered feasible if it can satisfy
\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{tot}} \ge \max\!\left(\gamma_T D_{\mathrm{low}},\; T_{\mathrm{req}}(V_{\mathrm{eff,req}})\right),
\end{equation}
where \(\gamma_T\) is the low-speed thrust margin factor. This is the key mechanism that prevents the optimizer from selecting an architecture that produces a large jet but not enough total thrust.

\subsection{Effective Oswald-factor calibration for the rectangular DAE51 wing}

The repository's Stage~1 drag model uses the standard induced-drag closure
\begin{equation}
K = \frac{1}{\pi e AR},
\end{equation}
inside a concept-level drag polar of the form
\begin{equation}
C_{D,\mathrm{tot}} \approx C_{D0} + K C_L^2.
\end{equation}
For the present rectangular wing,
\begin{equation}
AR = \frac{b^2}{S} = \frac{b^2}{bc} = \frac{b}{c} = 5.714.
\end{equation}
The earlier value \(e=0.8\) was a generic handbook placeholder. It has now been replaced by an implementation-matched effective value calibrated to the current DAE51 wing.

The calibration procedure uses a clean rectangular \SI{2.0}{m} by \SI{0.35}{m} wing built in AeroSandbox VLM together with DAE51 section drag from NeuralFoil. At each cruise/loiter speed, the section Reynolds number is
\begin{equation}
Re = \frac{\rho V c}{\mu}.
\end{equation}
For each sampled angle of attack on the pre-stall branch, the clean-wing total drag coefficient is assembled as
\begin{equation}
C_{D,\mathrm{tot}}(\alpha) =
C_{D,\mathrm{profile}}(\alpha) + C_{D,\mathrm{induced}}(\alpha),
\end{equation}
with \(C_{D,\mathrm{profile}}\) taken from the DAE51 NeuralFoil polar and \(C_{D,\mathrm{induced}}\) taken from the rectangular-wing VLM result. The resulting clean-wing drag polar is then fit in least-squares form as
\begin{equation}
C_{D,\mathrm{tot}} \approx C_{D0,\mathrm{fit}} + K_{\mathrm{fit}} C_L^2,
\end{equation}
so that the effective Oswald factor follows from
\begin{equation}
e_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{1}{\pi AR K_{\mathrm{fit}}}.
\end{equation}

This distinction matters. If one computes a pure VLM span-efficiency check directly from the induced drag alone,
\begin{equation}
e_{\mathrm{span}} = \frac{C_L^2}{\pi AR C_{D_i}},
\end{equation}
the present rectangular wing gives a value close to unity, as expected for a clean untwisted wing in a VLM setting. However, the Stage~1 repository model does not isolate pure span efficiency; it uses a single quadratic term to absorb both induced drag and the lift-dependent growth of the clean-wing total drag polar. For that reason the quantity needed by the Stage~1 model is the fitted \(e_{\mathrm{eff}}\), not the idealized \(e_{\mathrm{span}}\).

Across the \SIrange{7.5}{10.0}{m/s} cruise/loiter range, the DAE51 rectangular-wing fit produced
\begin{equation}
e_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 0.659.
\end{equation}
This is the value now used in the repository. It is therefore more wing-specific than the earlier handbook placeholder, while still remaining faithful to the simple Stage~1 polar structure.

\subsection{RPM closure and mass closure}

RPM is not treated as a free outer-loop design variable. Instead, the solver finds the minimum RPM that closes the low-speed and cruise conditions by bisection on the residuals
\begin{align}
R_{\mathrm{low}}(\mathrm{RPM}) &=
T_{\mathrm{tot}}(\mathrm{RPM}) -
\max\!\left(\gamma_{T,\mathrm{low}} D_{\mathrm{low}}(\mathrm{RPM}),\;
T_{\mathrm{req}}(V_{\mathrm{eff,req}}(\mathrm{RPM}))\right), \\
R_{\mathrm{cruise}}(\mathrm{RPM}) &=
T_{\mathrm{tot}}(\mathrm{RPM}) - \gamma_{T,\mathrm{cruise}} D_{\mathrm{cruise}}.
\end{align}
This matters because it collapses the Stage~1 architecture space onto physical operating points rather than allowing the Pareto front to be inflated by redundant throttle settings.

Once a candidate has solved low-speed and cruise RPM, the repository estimates motor, ESC, propeller, and battery mass. The battery closure is
\begin{align}
E_{\mathrm{climb}} &= P_{\mathrm{low}} \frac{t_{\mathrm{climb}}}{60}, \\
E_{\mathrm{loiter}} &= P_{\mathrm{cruise}} \frac{t_{\mathrm{loiter}}}{60}, \\
m_{\mathrm{battery}} &=
\frac{(E_{\mathrm{climb}} + E_{\mathrm{loiter}})(1+r_{\mathrm{reserve}})}
\varepsilon_{\mathrm{battery}}},
\end{align}
where \(\varepsilon_{\mathrm{battery}}\) is the battery specific energy in \si{Wh/kg}. The propulsion masses are fit as power laws to representative component datasets:
\begin{align}
m_{\mathrm{prop}} &\sim a_p D_p^{b_p}\left(1 + k_p \frac{P}{D}\right), \\
m_{\mathrm{motor}} &\sim a_m P_{\mathrm{peak}}^{b_m}, \\
m_{\mathrm{ESC}} &\sim a_e I_{\mathrm{cont}}^{b_e}.
\end{align}
These equations are used only to rank architectures and enforce a mass budget; they are not intended to replace final hardware selection.

\subsection{Objectives and constraints}

The present Stage~1 Pareto front uses three objectives:
\begin{align}
\min \; P_{\mathrm{low}}, \qquad
\min \; E_{\mathrm{loiter}}, \qquad
\min \; m_{\mathrm{propulsion}}.
\end{align}
Crucially, these objectives are \emph{not} combined into a weighted sum. The code in \texttt{optimizer/core/pareto.py} evaluates true nondominance: a feasible candidate \(i\) is removed only if there exists another feasible candidate \(j\) such that
\begin{align}
f_k^{(j)} &\le f_k^{(i)} \qquad \text{for } k\in\{1,2,3\}, \\
f_k^{(j)} &< f_k^{(i)} \qquad \text{for at least one } k,
\end{align}
with
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{f} =
\left(P_{\mathrm{low}},\, E_{\mathrm{loiter}},\, m_{\mathrm{propulsion}}\right).
\end{equation}
The returned front is later sorted lexicographically by this objective tuple, but that ordering affects only how the CSV and console report are printed; it does not alter front membership. This distinction matters for the interpretation of the selected concept: rank~6 is a later human-selected study point from the nondominated set, not the output of a hidden Stage~1 weighted score.

At the same time, the following constraints are enforced:
\begin{itemize}
\item propeller packing on the fixed \SI{2.0}{m} span, including fuselage clearance, inter-prop clearance, and tip margin,
\item low-speed \(V_{\mathrm{eff}} \ge V_{\mathrm{eff,req}}\),
\item positive thrust margin at low speed and cruise,
\item tip Mach margin,
\item disk-loading bounds,
\item mass budget closure.
\end{itemize}

The packing constraints are important. They prevent the optimizer from choosing impossible high-count, large-diameter combinations simply because they look attractive in a power-only model. This again reflects Hawkswell's point that propeller size and location are not independent and that the benefit of a smaller jet must be considered together with the wing's need to remain inside it.\cite{Hawkswell2022}

The model clearly distinguishes a top-lift cluster (S1210 and E423), a middle compromise (DAE51), and a weaker low-speed option (NACA~0012), but it should not be used to over-interpret a difference of only a few hundredths in \(C_L\) as if it were already experimentally resolved.

\section{Rectangular-Wing Control-Surface Sizing}

\subsection{Why motors in front of flaps matter}

The present control-surface study focuses on a rectangular wing of span \SI{2.0}{m} and chord \SI{0.35}{m} using DAE51 as the downstream section choice. The specific design question is not merely ``what flap and aileron fit on the wing?'' but rather ``what flap and aileron make sense when the inboard propellers are intended to blow the flap system?''

Agrawal et al. provide the main physical motivation for this emphasis. Their section-response and wake results show that the blown slotted-flap system benefits from injecting higher total pressure into the flap region and turning the jet downward through the flap, thereby increasing lift while also modifying pitching moment.\cite{Agrawal2019} In other words, the flap is not an isolated high-lift device; it is part of a jet/flap system. That is why the present repository no longer allows the flap span to drift to the smallest-area solution. Instead, the flap candidates are penalized if they fail to cover enough of the inboard propeller array.

\subsection{Common-\(\alpha\) whole-wing assembly}

The repository now assembles the whole-wing lift and pitching-moment curves from blown and unblown regions at common \(\alpha\). In schematic form,
\begin{equation}
C_L(\alpha)
=
\frac{1}{q_\infty S}\sum_k q_k S_k c_{l,k}(\alpha),
\end{equation}
and similarly,
\begin{equation}
C_M(\alpha)
=
\frac{1}{q_\infty S c}\sum_k q_k S_k c_{m,k}(\alpha),
\end{equation}
where the index \(k\) spans the clean-unblown, clean-blown, flap-unblown, and flap-blown subareas. This is the crucial step that keeps the project from confusing a large section \(c_l\) with an equally large whole-wing \(C_L\).

The code implements the blown-strip part of this assembly in two steps. First, an immersed blown-strip coefficient is formed from the local blown and unblown section response:
\begin{equation}
\tilde c_{l,b}^{\,\mathrm{raw}} =
c_{l,u} +
\chi\left[\left(\frac{q_b}{q_\infty}\right)c_{l,b} - c_{l,u}\right],
\end{equation}
where \(\chi\) is the immersion factor described below. Second, the freestream-referenced blown-strip contribution is softly limited to remain within the same order of magnitude as the section-level blown-flap data:
\begin{equation}
\tilde c_{l,b} =
c_{l,\mathrm{lim}}\;
\tanh\!\left(\frac{\tilde c_{l,b}^{\,\mathrm{raw}}}{c_{l,\mathrm{lim}}}\right).
\end{equation}
The same scale factor is applied to the corresponding blown-strip \(c_d\) and \(c_m\). In the repository this step is used to prevent the whole-wing assembly from reporting obviously inflated values that would arise if the local section coefficients were multiplied by the full dynamic-pressure ratio without any bounded interpretation.

This correction was introduced after an earlier reporting bug made the whole-wing \(C_L\) unrealistically large by effectively multiplying a maximum obtainable unblown section value by the full velocity-ratio scaling. The present implementation instead evaluates the clean and blown section responses at the actual common \(\alpha\) of the flight condition and then assembles the full wing from those strip contributions. This is much closer to the logic of both Hawkswell's two-dimensional immersed-strip treatment and Agrawal's section-response framework.\cite{Hawkswell2022,Agrawal2019}

The section polars themselves are built from three ingredients:
\begin{enumerate}
\item NeuralFoil-based clean and flap-deflected section response,
\item a staged slotted-flap correction that separates low-angle lift-curve displacement from near-stall augmentation,
\item the Cambridge-style blown-strip immersion weighting and the bounded over-drop stall penalty described later.
\end{enumerate}

The slotted-flap correction is also implementation-matched rather than purely conceptual. The code begins with the clean and flap-deflected NeuralFoil curves, then applies a staged increment
\begin{equation}
\Delta c_l(\alpha) =
\Delta c_{l,0} G_{\mathrm{low}}(\alpha) +
\Delta c_{l,\max} G_{\mathrm{peak}}(\alpha),
\end{equation}
with
\begin{align}
\Delta c_{l,0} &\propto \left(\frac{c_f}{c}\right)\sin\delta_f, \\
\Delta c_{l,\max} &\propto
\left(\frac{c_f}{c}\right)^{0.8}\sin^{0.95}\delta_f,
\end{align}
where \(G_{\mathrm{low}}\) and \(G_{\mathrm{peak}}\) are smooth low-angle and near-peak gates. This allows the model to increase low-angle camber-like lift and near-stall flap benefit separately, which proved necessary once the flap deflection reached \(40^\circ\).

The underlying roll-rate relation is the standard quasi-steady balance between aileron rolling moment and roll damping. In coefficient form,
\begin{equation}
\hat p_{\mathrm{ss}} = -\frac{C_{l_{\delta_a}}\delta_a}{C_{l_{\hat p}}},
\end{equation}
which the code converts to a dimensional roll-rate estimate through
\begin{equation}
\dot\phi_{\mathrm{ss}} \approx
\hat p_{\mathrm{ss}}\frac{2V}{b}.
\end{equation}
In cruise this relation is evaluated directly from the AeroSandbox VLM derivatives. In the low-speed blown case the same structure is retained, but the derivatives are scaled by blown-overlap fraction and local dynamic-pressure ratio to produce the explicitly labeled roll-rate proxy.

\section{Motor Height Trade: Clean Wing Versus Slotted-Flap Wing}

\subsection{Immersion and over-drop penalty}

The present motor-height trade combines two mechanisms.

The first is the Cambridge-style immersion criterion. In the code this is implemented as a smooth immersion factor that tends toward unity when the wing remains within the jet and tends toward zero when the jet rises above the wing. This is the direct implementation of Hawkswell et al.'s central observation that the wing should remain submerged in the jet at the operating incidence.\cite{Hawkswell2022}

The implementation begins by defining an available submergence,
\begin{equation}
h_{\mathrm{avail}} = \frac{1}{2}H_j D_p + \Delta z_p,
\end{equation}
where \(H_j\) is the jet-core height factor, \(D_p\) is propeller diameter, and \(\Delta z_p\) is motor drop. The required submergence for the clean strip is
\begin{equation}
h_{\mathrm{req,c}} =
c\left(\beta_0 + \beta_\alpha \sin\alpha\right)a_x,
\end{equation}
and for the flap strip
\begin{equation}
h_{\mathrm{req,f}} =
h_{\mathrm{req,c}} +
c\,\beta_f \left(\frac{c_f}{c}\right)\sin\delta_f.
\end{equation}
The axial factor is
\begin{equation}
a_x = 1 + 0.35\frac{\max(|x_p/c|-0.25,0)}{0.25}.
\end{equation}
The final immersion factors are
\begin{align}
\chi_c &= \frac{1}{2}\left[1+\tanh\left(\frac{h_{\mathrm{avail}}-h_{\mathrm{req,c}}}{\ell_\chi}\right)\right], \\
\chi_f &= \frac{1}{2}\left[1+\tanh\left(\frac{h_{\mathrm{avail}}-h_{\mathrm{req,f}}}{\ell_\chi}\right)\right],
\end{align}
where \(\ell_\chi\) is the jet-softness length scale. These equations are used directly in the whole-wing assembly to determine how much of the blown-strip benefit survives at each \(\alpha\).

The second is a bounded over-drop penalty. This is \emph{not} a paper-derived formula; it is a paper-informed surrogate introduced to capture the qualitative MIT/Agrawal-style observation that overly aggressive jet geometry can make the high-lift system stall more suddenly.\cite{Agrawal2019} The penalty is intentionally weak at low angle of attack and becomes active mainly near the peak-lift and post-stall region.

The severity scalar begins with a nondimensional motor-drop ratio \(h=\Delta z_p/c\):
\begin{equation}
s_0 =
\left[
\max\!\left(
\frac{h-h_{\mathrm{on}}}{h_{\mathrm{ref}}-h_{\mathrm{on}}},
0\right)
\right]^{p}
\left[\max\!\left(\frac{V_{\mathrm{eff}}}{V_\infty}-1,0\right)\right]^{q},
\end{equation}
which is then bounded as
\begin{equation}
s = \frac{s_0}{1+s_0}.
\end{equation}
If the flap is active, the code multiplies this severity by a relief factor to represent the fact that the slotted-flap system tolerates drop somewhat better than the clean strip. The penalty then shifts the apparent peak to lower angle of attack and steepens the post-stall decay:
\begin{align}
\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}} &= \alpha + K_\alpha s G_{\mathrm{stall}}(\alpha), \\
c_l^\star &=
\mathcal{I}[c_l](\alpha_{\mathrm{eff}})
\left(1-K_{cl}sG_{\mathrm{stall}}\right)
\exp\!\left(-K_{\mathrm{post}} s G_{\mathrm{post}}\right),
\end{align}
where \(\mathcal{I}[c_l]\) denotes interpolation on the original polar and \(G_{\mathrm{stall}}, G_{\mathrm{post}}\) are smooth gates that activate near and beyond the peak-lift region. The code applies analogous penalties to \(c_d\) and \(c_m\). The purpose of these equations is not to claim a final stall model, but to ensure the motor-height trade can express the literature-backed ``too high loses immersion, too low stalls more abruptly'' trend.

\subsection{Trade setup}

The motor-height study holds the selected rank~6 propulsion architecture, flap geometry, and aileron geometry fixed while sweeping
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta z_p}{c} \in \{0.00, 0.04, 0.08, 0.12, 0.16, 0.20, 0.24\}.
\end{equation}
Two scenario branches are evaluated:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a clean blown wing with no flap deployed,
\item a slotted flap plus blowing.
\end{enumerate}

This split is important because the present project does not assume that the no-flap and flap cases respond identically to motor height. Agrawal et al. strongly suggest otherwise by showing that the flap, motor-axis angle, and blowing level interact in the stall behaviour itself.\cite{Agrawal2019}

\subsection{Motor-height results}

The results of the trade are shown in Figs.~\ref{fig:motor-metrics} and \ref{fig:motor-overlay}. Both branches show the same three-stage behaviour:
\begin{enumerate}
\item too high: insufficient immersion, reduced blown benefit,
\item moderate drop: recovered immersion, near-optimal lift,
\item too low: over-drop penalty activates, stall sharpens, benefit degrades.
\end{enumerate}

For the clean blown wing, the best equivalent stall speed occurs near a drop of \SI{42}{mm} (\(\Delta z_p/c = 0.12\)) and the blown-wing benefit saturates by about \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.08\). For the slotted-flap branch, the optimum is similarly near \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.12\), but the degradation beyond that point is somewhat more gradual. This is physically plausible: the flap system can tolerate somewhat more jet immersion before the upper-surface or post-flap flow begins to deteriorate. These values now come from the DAE51-centered unified workflow rather than from the earlier generic rank-6 study.

Numerically, the current trade gives the following representative results:
\begin{itemize}
\item clean blown wing at \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.12\): \(C_{L,\max}\approx 5.85\), \(V_s\approx \SI{4.42}{m/s}\),
\item slotted flap plus blowing at \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.12\): \(C_{L,\max}\approx 5.81\), \(V_s\approx \SI{4.44}{m/s}\),
\item neither branch meets the \SI{4.0}{m/s} target under the current assumptions.
\end{itemize}

The present project therefore supports a simple design statement: motor drop should be large enough to keep the flap/wing system immersed in the jet, but not so large that the stall behaviour becomes unacceptably abrupt.

\section{Unified DAE51 Workflow and Frozen-Geometry Speed Sweep}

The current repository no longer treats the airfoil comparison, control sizing, motor-height trade, and off-design speed evaluation as loosely connected side studies. Instead, \texttt{optimizer/core/wing\_workflow.py} now runs a single downstream sequence:
\begin{enumerate}
\item multi-airfoil front-end section analysis,
\item DAE51 rectangular-wing control-surface sizing at the low-speed design point,
\item DAE51 motor-height trade on the frozen control geometry,
\item frozen-geometry DAE51 speed sweep from \SIrange{5}{15}{m/s} in \SI{0.5}{m/s} increments.
\end{enumerate}
The design passed into this workflow is the same one reported above: flap span fraction 0.65, flap chord fraction 0.34, aileron span fraction 0.28, aileron chord fraction 0.28, and optimized motor drop \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.12\).

The speed sweep evaluates three flap states at every speed,
\begin{equation}
\delta_f \in \{0^\circ, 20^\circ, 40^\circ\},
\end{equation}
and re-solves RPM at each condition rather than carrying the original low-speed blown velocity forward unchanged. In implementation-matched form, the off-design solve seeks the smallest RPM such that both lift and thrust closure are satisfied:
\begin{equation}
R_{\mathrm{sweep}}(\mathrm{RPM})
=
\min\!\left(
C_{L,\max}(\mathrm{RPM})-C_{L,\mathrm{req}},
T_{\mathrm{tot}}(\mathrm{RPM})-D_{\mathrm{req}}(\mathrm{RPM})
\right).
\end{equation}
This formulation matters because it makes \(V_{\mathrm{eff}}\) condition-dependent through the solved thrust level:
\begin{align}
v_i(\mathrm{RPM}) &= \sqrt{\frac{T_{\mathrm{tot}}(\mathrm{RPM})}{2\rho A_{\mathrm{disk}}}}, \\
V_{\mathrm{eff}}(\mathrm{RPM}) &= V_\infty + 2v_i(\mathrm{RPM}).
\end{align}
The current speed-sweep plots therefore show a physically changing blown velocity rather than a low-speed value that has been frozen and merely re-plotted at other freestream speeds.

The off-design results are shown in Figs.~\ref{fig:sweep-performance} and \ref{fig:sweep-operating}. The main trend is clear. The \(\delta_f=40^\circ\) branch remains the strongest pure high-lift branch in terms of \(C_{L,\max}\), peaking near \SI{5}{m/s} at about \(C_{L,\max}\approx 5.87\), but it is also the most drag-intensive branch and remains thrust-limited throughout the current sweep. The \(\delta_f=20^\circ\) branch is intermediate: it becomes feasible only in a narrower upper-speed band. The \(\delta_f=0^\circ\) branch carries most of the feasible corridor because its drag remains low enough that the re-solved RPM can cover both the lift requirement and the drag requirement over much of the mid- to high-speed range.

At \SI{10}{m/s}, for example, the clean branch solves near \(\mathrm{RPM}\approx 6251\), giving \(V_{\mathrm{eff}}\approx \SI{12.38}{m/s}\) and \(v_i\approx \SI{1.19}{m/s}\). Its whole-wing \(C_{L,\max}\approx 1.82\) remains above the required \(C_L\approx 1.14\), but the branch is still marked infeasible because the thrust deficit is slightly negative under the current closure. By contrast, the \(\delta_f=20^\circ\) and \(40^\circ\) branches at \SI{10}{m/s} are no longer lift-limited at all; they are drag-limited and thrust-limited. This is exactly the kind of operating-point distinction the unified workflow was intended to reveal.

\section{Operating-Point Implications and Representative Motor Targeting}

\subsection{What the current concept can do}

The present selected concept is now well enough defined to state its credible operating envelope under the current model:
\begin{itemize}
\item low-speed blown condition near \SI{4.44}{m/s} equivalent stall speed rather than the target \SI{4.0}{m/s},
\item cruise at \SI{10}{m/s} with electrical power near \SI{131}{W},
\item loiter energy near \SI{39.35}{Wh} for the current \SI{18}{min} requirement,
\item low-speed blown velocity margin of about \SI{5.15}{m/s} relative to the Stage~1 required value,
\item high-lift wing-only pitching moment near \(C_M=-0.68\) at the peak-lift point.
\end{itemize}

This is a very useful result. The selected architecture is not energetically struggling; it is high-lift limited. In other words, the current concept is already close to the energy and propulsion regime it needs, but still short of the low-speed aerodynamic requirement.

\subsection{Representative motor and ESC targets}

The repository now converts the selected Stage~1 operating point into a representative motor target sheet. For the selected rank~6 architecture, the present required per-motor values are:
\begin{align}
P_{\mathrm{shaft,peak}} &\approx \SI{11.6}{W}, \\
P_{\mathrm{elec,peak}} &\approx \SI{17.1}{W}, \\
I_{\mathrm{peak}} &\approx \SI{1.16}{A}, \\
K_V &\approx \SI{771}{rpm/V}.
\end{align}
The current target band is widened heuristically to about \SIrange{655}{886}{rpm/V}. The representative local dataset currently identifies a SunnySky A2212 \SI{1000}{Kv} class motor and an \SI{8}{A} BLHeli-S ESC class as the nearest class-level matches.

The motor-targeting stage computes these values from the selected Stage~1 operating point using
\begin{align}
P_{\mathrm{elec,peak}} &= \max(P_{\mathrm{elec,low}},P_{\mathrm{elec,cruise}})/N_p, \\
I_{\mathrm{peak}} &= \frac{P_{\mathrm{elec,peak}}}{V_{\mathrm{batt}}}, \\
K_{V,\mathrm{req}} &= \frac{\mathrm{RPM}_{\mathrm{low}}}{0.85 V_{\mathrm{batt}}},
\end{align}
with heuristic procurement targets
\begin{align}
P_{\mathrm{elec,target}} &= 1.25\,P_{\mathrm{elec,peak}}, \\
I_{\mathrm{cont,target}} &= 1.25\,I_{\mathrm{peak}}.
\end{align}
These equations are used only to turn the aerodynamic operating point into a practical hardware search band. They are intentionally conservative because the local motor database is class-based rather than a final vendor-backed downselect.

\section{Design Considerations for the Aircraft at the Present Stage}

The current repository is now mature enough to say something concrete about what the airplane should look like and what design moves remain most valuable.

\subsection{The propulsion concept}

The \(10\times 5.5\times 2.2\)~in balanced architecture remains a defensible study baseline because it combines:
\begin{itemize}
\item low cruise power relative to the rest of the Pareto set,
\item the highest momentum coefficient in the screened family,
\item enough propeller count to distribute blowing spanwise,
\item small enough diameter to package ten propellers on the \SI{2.0}{m} span.
\end{itemize}
This does not mean the concept is globally optimal. It means it is a good architecture around which to resolve the remaining aerodynamic questions.

There is also a useful paper-level geometric coincidence here. For the present rectangular chord \(c=\SI{0.35}{m}\), Hawkswell et al.'s nacelle-inclusive optimum diameter scale of \(0.39c\) corresponds to about \SI{5.37}{in}, which is remarkably close to the selected \(5.5\)~in diameter.\cite{Hawkswell2022} Similarly, the current repository motor-drop choice of \(0.12c\) and the axial location \(x_p/c=-0.25\) mirror the same Cambridge practical optimum. This does not prove the selected architecture is globally optimal, but it does mean the manually retained Stage~1 concept lands in a geometry neighborhood that is independently supported by the Cambridge study.

\subsection{The wing}

The fixed-span geometry study suggests that the aerodynamic planform would prefer:
\begin{itemize}
\item somewhat larger wing area than the original \SI{0.70}{m^2} rectangular baseline,
\item moderate taper (\(\lambda \approx 0.62\)),
\item modest washout (about \(-2.5^\circ\)).
\end{itemize}
However, the control-surface study indicates that even the current rectangular wing with aggressive blown slotted flaps only reaches \(C_{L,\max}\approx 5.81\). This has an immediate implication: if \SI{4.0}{m/s} remains a hard mission requirement, either the wing must become larger, the mass must come down, or the local high-lift system must improve further.

Using the rectangular-wing control result,
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{req}} \approx \frac{W}{q_\infty C_{L,\max}},
\end{equation}
the current \(C_{L,\max}\approx 5.81\) implies that the present \SI{5.0}{kg} airplane would require roughly
\begin{equation}
S_{\mathrm{req}} \approx \SI{0.86}{m^2}
\end{equation}
to hold \SI{4}{m/s}, which is about 23\% larger than the original rectangular wing and about 16\% larger than the current rank~6 Stage~3 refined wing. Conversely, keeping the original \SI{0.70}{m^2} wing but limiting the airplane to \(C_{L,\max}\approx 5.81\) implies a mass closer to \SI{4.06}{kg} if the \SI{4}{m/s} requirement is to be met. This is one of the clearest design outputs of the project.

\subsection{The high-lift system}

The selected flap is already large and strongly integrated with the propeller layout:
\begin{itemize}
\item \(65\%\) semispan,
\item \(c_f/c = 0.34\),
\item \(\delta_f = 40^\circ\),
\item overlap with 8 of the 10 propeller disks.
\end{itemize}
This means the project has already exhausted the most obvious ``just make the flap bigger'' move. Future improvements in low-speed capability are therefore more likely to come from improved section choice, improved slot geometry, better flap kinematics, or a modest increase in wing area than from merely extending the flap further.

Agrawal et al. are especially useful here because they explain why the motor-over-flap arrangement is so powerful in the first place.\cite{Agrawal2019} The flap is not only a conventional camber-increasing device. It is also the surface that receives the injected high-total-pressure jet through the slot, delays main-element wake bursting, delays flap separation, and turns the jet downward so that the vertical momentum change contributes directly to lift. That is the aerodynamic reason the present solver penalizes flap geometries that do not place enough propeller disks over the flap.

\subsection{Motor height}

The present motor-height trade supports a drop near \(0.08c\) to \(0.12c\). That range is consistent with the paper-backed scale from Hawkswell et al. and with the present control study. There is no strong evidence in the current results that pushing the motor much lower than that is beneficial. Indeed, the present over-drop penalty suggests that such a change would likely sharpen stall before it helps the mean lift level.

\subsection{Controls and trim}

The current blown flap does not come for free. The wing-only pitching moment near the high-lift point is strongly negative, and the low-speed aileron authority, while still viable, is weaker than the cruise condition. The design implication is straightforward: the next aircraft-level study should not only optimize \(C_L\), but also ensure that the empennage and CG envelope can absorb the trim penalty associated with the blown slotted-flap system.

\subsection{Airframe and systems integration notes}

Several aircraft-level notes emerged alongside the solver development and are worth recording explicitly even though they are not yet full optimization variables.
\begin{itemize}
\item The wing will likely need to sit relatively far aft on the fuselage so that the propeller plane, flap system, and CG placement can coexist without excessive fuselage interference. This is a packaging and structural recommendation rather than a direct solver output.
\item The motor mounts should remain modular. Agrawal et al. demonstrated that both motor height and motor-axis angle alter stall behavior,\cite{Agrawal2019} so a practical prototype should allow discrete changes in drop and axis angle rather than fixing one rigid mount from the outset.
\item The present solver applies a common motor-drop geometry to propellers lying over the flap span. A more hardware-specific future design may instead lower only the most inboard propellers per side, leaving the outer leading-edge propellers on the wing centerline. That discrete installation has not yet been modeled explicitly and should therefore be treated as a build concept rather than a solved result.
\item Low-speed stability will likely require full differential-thrust authority. The current avionics/GNC implication is that the flight computer and output expansion should support mapping each ESC independently rather than treating the propeller line as a single thrust channel.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and Recommended Next Steps}

The present workflow is coherent, but it is not the final answer. The main limitations are:
\begin{itemize}
\item Stage~1 propeller aerodynamics remain surrogate-based rather than tied to a specific measured propeller family.
\item The blown-strip response is paper-informed but not yet quantitatively fitted to a dedicated data set for the specific geometry being studied.
\item The motor-height over-drop penalty is qualitative, not experimentally fitted.
\item The rectangular-wing control study still uses a low-speed aileron proxy rather than a full lateral-directional model.
\item The representative motor target stage is class-based and uses local datasets rather than a live vendor-backed search.
\item The current whole-wing airfoil comparison compresses some of the true airfoil-to-airfoil differences because it holds propulsor layout, flap geometry, immersion logic, and blown-area fractions fixed across all candidates. It is therefore stronger as a clustering and ranking tool than as a final arbiter of very small performance gaps.
\item The new off-design speed sweep is valuable precisely because it distinguishes lift-limited from thrust-limited branches, but it is still a frozen-geometry branch comparison rather than a full mission control-schedule optimization.
\end{itemize}

The most valuable next steps are therefore:
\begin{enumerate}
\item replace the coarse Stage~1 propeller surrogates with measured propeller data for the candidate \(5.5\)~in and \(6.0\)~in classes,
\item calibrate the motor-height and blown-section surrogates directly against a section data set in the spirit of Agrawal et al.,
\item carry the flap-induced \(C_M\) burden into a whole-aircraft trim and tail-sizing study,
\item test whether modest increases in wing area or reductions in mass are a more efficient way to reach the \SI{4.0}{m/s} requirement than pursuing still more aggressive blown lift,
\item if an operational preference must eventually be imposed on the Stage~1 shortlist, add a \emph{post-Pareto} weighted utility or ranking layer rather than replacing the current nondominated filter itself.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusions}

This project has produced a usable, coherent design workflow for a blown-wing aircraft concept. The main findings are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The Stage~1 Pareto optimizer is doing useful work. It is not merely sweeping propeller parameters; it is exposing the fundamental trade between cruise power, jet authority, and geometric coverage. Just as importantly, it is now explicit that Stage~1 uses true three-objective nondominance rather than a hidden weighted score.
\item The selected \(10\times 5.5\times 2.2\)~in balanced architecture was retained for good reason. It was a manual downselection from the nondominated set because it sits near the low-cruise-power edge of the Pareto front while also preserving the highest momentum coefficient in the screened family, making it a strong candidate for downstream blown-wing study.
\item Large section \(c_l\) values from blown-flap literature should not be interpreted directly as whole-aircraft \(C_L\). Once the wing is assembled at common \(\alpha\), the present selected concept reaches a more realistic \(C_{L,\max}\approx 5.8\), not 8--9.
\item The present rectangular-wing DAE51 study supports a large inboard single-slotted flap and a viable outboard aileron, but the concept still falls short of the \SI{4.0}{m/s} requirement under the current mass and wing area assumptions.
\item Motors in front of flaps matter not simply because they add momentum, but because they create a jet/flap system. That system increases lift, deepens the wing-only pitching moment, and makes motor height a genuine high-lift design variable.
\item The current motor-height trade is physically richer than the earlier bonus-factor model. It now captures both the benefit of recovering jet immersion and the degradation associated with overly aggressive motor drop.
\item The new unified workflow is already useful. The multi-airfoil front-end preserves the comparative airfoil study, while the DAE51 deep-dive makes the control-surface, motor-height, and off-design speed-sweep outputs internally consistent. The speed sweep further shows that flap deployment is not automatically beneficial off-design: the \(\delta_f=40^\circ\) branch remains lift-rich but drag- and thrust-limited, whereas the clean branch carries most of the feasible corridor.
\item The present operating point is not dominated by propulsion power or battery energy. The main challenge that remains is still low-speed aerodynamic capability and the trim burden that accompanies it.
\end{enumerate}

In that sense, the project has done what a good design-space study should do: it has narrowed the uncertainty. The current concept is not yet the final airplane, but it is now clear where the remaining leverage lies --- in wing area, mass, high-lift effectiveness, and trim --- and where it does not.
