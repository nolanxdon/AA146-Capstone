\begin{abstract}
This note documents the present implementation of the blown-wing concept optimizer and the associated rectangular-wing high-lift sizing workflow for a distributed-propulsion aircraft. Stage~1 performs architecture screening over propeller count, diameter, pitch ratio, and propeller-family surrogates while solving the operating RPMs internally. A downselected concept --- rank~6 in the current shortlist, corresponding to a \(10\times 5.5\times 2.2\ \mathrm{in}\) balanced propeller arrangement --- is then carried into a higher-fidelity rectangular-wing study with single-slotted flaps, cruise ailerons, and a limited motor-height sensitivity sweep. The high-lift model has been revised to distinguish between section-level blown-flap response and whole-wing freestream-referenced lift. Following the structure of recent blown-wing studies, the blown portion of the wing is treated as a nominally uniform two-dimensional strip, while the final aircraft-level \(C_L\) is recovered by area-weighting blown and unblown regions. This distinction proves essential when interpreting section plots that reach \(c_l\approx 9\): for the current rank-6 concept, the corresponding whole-wing \(C_{L,\max}\) remains near 6 because only a fraction of the wing is both blown and flapped. The note is intentionally implementation-matched; all equations and figures correspond to the current repository outputs.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Distributed electric propulsion enables the propeller slipstream to be treated not only as a thrust source but also as a high-lift device. Recent experimental and design studies have shown that substantial low-speed lift augmentation can be obtained when a flap-equipped wing remains immersed in the propeller jet over the relevant operating range.\cite{Agrawal2019,Hawkswell2022} At the same time, the resulting design problem is strongly coupled: propeller diameter, spanwise placement, flap extent, and motor height all influence the slipstream footprint and therefore the usable high-lift benefit.

The present study seeks a propulsion architecture and high-lift arrangement that can support very low-speed flight on a fixed-span wing while retaining a practical cruise condition. The repository now contains three connected levels of modeling:
\begin{enumerate}
\item a Stage~1 coarse architecture screen,
\item a rectangular-wing slotted-flap and aileron sizing workflow for a chosen propulsion concept, and
\item a first-order motor-height trade study based on that same concept.
\end{enumerate}

Particular attention is given to the distinction between section response and wing-level performance. A two-dimensional blown section may attain \(c_l\) values near the upper end of the range reported in blown-flap experiments, yet the corresponding aircraft-level \(C_L\) can remain materially lower once the blown and unblown portions of the wing are mixed together. The present note therefore documents the current implementation in a form suitable for direct comparison with the generated repository artifacts.

\section{Stage~1 Architecture Screen}

\subsection{Design variables and fixed assumptions}

Stage~1 evaluates the discrete design space
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{x} =
\left\{
N_p,\;
D,\;
\frac{P}{D},\;
f_{\mathrm{prop}}
\right\},
\end{equation}
with
\begin{align}
N_p &\in \{4,6,8,10,12,14,16\}, \\
D &\in \{4.0,4.5,5.0,5.5,6.0,6.5,7.0,7.5,8.0,9.0,10.0\}\ \mathrm{in}, \\
\frac{P}{D} &\in \{0.40,0.50,0.60,0.70,0.80,0.90\}, \\
f_{\mathrm{prop}} &\in \{\texttt{high\_thrust},\texttt{balanced},\texttt{cruise}\}.
\end{align}

The global mission assumptions used in the present implementation are:
\begin{itemize}
\item gross mass \(m_{\mathrm{gross}} = \SI{5.0}{kg}\),
\item span \(b = \SI{2.0}{m}\),
\item baseline chord \(c = \SI{0.35}{m}\),
\item low-speed design condition \(V_{\infty,\mathrm{low}} = \SI{4.0}{m/s}\),
\item cruise condition \(V_{\infty,\mathrm{cruise}} = \SI{10.0}{m/s}\),
\item battery voltage \(V_{\mathrm{batt}} = \SI{14.8}{V}\),
\item battery specific energy \(\epsilon_{\mathrm{batt}} = \SI{180}{Wh/kg}\), and
\item wing-system working ceiling \(C_{L,\mathrm{ceil}} = 2.0\).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Propeller surrogate and operating-point solve}

The propeller surrogate remains a coarse architecture model. For a given propeller family, pitch ratio, and advance ratio \(J\),
\begin{equation}
J = \frac{V}{nD},
\qquad
n = \frac{\mathrm{RPM}}{60},
\end{equation}
the code constructs effective \(C_T\) and \(C_P\) values and then computes
\begin{align}
T_{\mathrm{per}} &= C_T \rho n^2 D^4, \\
P_{\mathrm{shaft,per}} &= C_P \rho n^3 D^5.
\end{align}
Total thrust and shaft power are
\begin{align}
T_{\mathrm{tot}} &= N_p T_{\mathrm{per}}, \\
P_{\mathrm{shaft,tot}} &= N_p P_{\mathrm{shaft,per}}.
\end{align}

Unlike the earliest grid-based version, RPM is no longer treated as an outer design variable. Stage~1 solves the low-speed and cruise RPMs internally with bisection so that each architecture is compared at its minimum feasible operating point.

\subsection{Blown-wing low-speed model}

The wing is partitioned into blown and unblown areas:
\begin{align}
S_b &= \eta_b S, \\
S_u &= (1-\eta_b)S.
\end{align}
The total disk area is
\begin{equation}
A_{\mathrm{disk}} = N_p \pi \left(\frac{D}{2}\right)^2.
\end{equation}
Stage~1 uses an actuator-disk relation to estimate the effective blown velocity:
\begin{align}
v_i &= \sqrt{\frac{T_{\mathrm{tot}}}{2 \rho A_{\mathrm{disk}}}}, \\
V_{\mathrm{eff}} &= V_\infty + 2v_i.
\end{align}
For the selected rank-6 concept discussed later,
\begin{equation}
V_{\mathrm{eff}} = \SI{14.622}{m/s}, \qquad
v_i = \SI{5.311}{m/s},
\end{equation}
at the low-speed condition \(V_\infty = \SI{4.0}{m/s}\).

Stage~1 also defines a wing-reference momentum coefficient,
\begin{equation}
C_\mu = \frac{T_{\mathrm{tot}}}{q_\infty S},
\end{equation}
and uses it in a clipped blown-section lift-augmentation model. However, because the current wing-system ceiling is held to \(C_{L,\mathrm{ceil}} = 2.0\), the dominant benefit of blowing at low speed is not a large rise in local section \(C_l\), but the increase in local dynamic pressure on the blown region.

\section{Rectangular-Wing High-Lift and Control-Surface Sizing}

\subsection{Configuration and assumptions}

The follow-on sizing study freezes the planform to a rectangular wing with
\begin{equation}
b = \SI{2.0}{m},
\qquad
c = \SI{0.35}{m},
\qquad
S = \SI{0.70}{m^2}.
\end{equation}
The selected propulsion concept is the current rank-6 architecture:
\begin{equation}
10 \times 5.5 \times 2.2\ \mathrm{in},
\end{equation}
with a balanced propeller family and three-blade metadata.

At low speed, the slotted flap is assumed to be fully deployed at its maximum deflection. In the current implementation, that means
\begin{equation}
\delta_f = \SI{40}{deg}.
\end{equation}
The propellers that lie ahead of the flap span are also assumed to sit slightly below the wing chord line. The present baseline drop is
\begin{equation}
\Delta z_p = 0.12 c = \SI{0.0420}{m} \approx 0.30 D_p,
\end{equation}
with the actuator disk placed at
\begin{equation}
x_p/c = -0.25.
\end{equation}
These values were chosen to be consistent with the Cambridge configuration study for the case in which real installation geometry matters.\cite{Hawkswell2022}

\subsection{Section-polar construction}

The current rectangular-wing workflow evaluates clean and control-deflected section polars with NeuralFoil, then applies a surrogate single-slotted-flap correction that is intentionally separated into low-\(\alpha\) and near-stall contributions. For the flapped section,
\begin{equation}
c_{l,f}(\alpha)
=
c_{l,\mathrm{NF}}(\alpha)
+
\Delta c_{l,0}\, g_0(\alpha)
+
\Delta c_{l,\max}\, g_{\max}(\alpha),
\end{equation}
where \(c_{l,\mathrm{NF}}\) is the NeuralFoil prediction for the deflected control surface, \(g_0\) weights the low-angle lift-curve displacement, and \(g_{\max}\) weights the near-stall lift increment. Analogous corrections are applied to \(c_d\) and \(c_m\),
\begin{align}
c_{d,f}(\alpha)
&=
c_{d,\mathrm{NF}}(\alpha)
+
\Delta c_{d,0}\, h_0(\alpha)
+
\Delta c_{d,\max}\, h_{\max}(\alpha), \\
c_{m,f}(\alpha)
&=
c_{m,\mathrm{NF}}(\alpha)
+
\Delta c_{m,0}\, m_0(\alpha)
+
\Delta c_{m,\max}\, m_{\max}(\alpha).
\end{align}

This formulation was adopted to avoid using the raw \SI{40}{deg} NeuralFoil flap polar directly as the post-stall high-lift truth model. In the present implementation, the flap correction is still a concept-level surrogate; however, it now reproduces the expected qualitative behavior more clearly than the earlier single-gain formulation by allowing the flap to improve both the low-angle lift curve and the near-stall response. Motor height is not applied directly inside these section polars. Instead, it enters at the wing-assembly level through a jet-immersion model.

\subsection{Section response and whole-wing response}

The section plots generated in the airfoil-comparison stage are two-dimensional blown-strip plots. They are intended to be compared against measurements such as those of Agrawal et al.\cite{Agrawal2019} and therefore are reported as section quantities \(c_l\), \(c_d\), and \(c_m\) at constant \(C_\mu\). The whole-wing model used for the selected concept is different. The wing is divided into four regions: clean/unblown, flap/unblown, clean/blown, and flap/blown. The total freestream-referenced lift coefficient is then assembled at common \(\alpha\) as
\begin{equation}
C_L(\alpha)
=
f_{cu}\, c_{l,cu}(\alpha)
+
f_{fu}\, c_{l,fu}(\alpha,\delta_f)
+
f_{cb}\, \tilde{c}_{l,cb}(\alpha,C_\mu)
+
f_{fb}\, \tilde{c}_{l,fb}(\alpha,\delta_f,C_\mu,\Delta z_p),
\end{equation}
where the \(f_i\) are wing-area fractions and the tilde denotes the blown-strip contribution after freestream re-referencing and the present calibration limit on usable blown-section lift.

The present motor-height treatment follows the key lesson of Hawkswell et al.: the blown-wing benefit is maintained only while the wing remains submerged in the jet.\cite{Hawkswell2022} To represent this in a low-order form, separate clean-strip and flap-strip immersion margins are defined as
\begin{align}
m_c(\alpha)
&=
\frac{D_p}{2}
+
\Delta z_p
-
c\left[
\beta_0
+
\beta_\alpha \sin\alpha
\right], \\
m_f(\alpha,\delta_f)
&=
\frac{D_p}{2}
+
\Delta z_p
-
c\left[
\beta_0
+
\beta_\alpha \sin\alpha
+
\beta_f \frac{c_f}{c}\sin\delta_f
\right],
\end{align}
with the current implementation using
\begin{equation}
\beta_0 = 0.08,\qquad
\beta_\alpha = 0.55,\qquad
\beta_f = 0.18.
\end{equation}
These constants should not be interpreted as values fitted directly from the literature. The literature supports the structure of the model and the relevant motor-height scale, but not the exact numerical values above. In particular, Hawkswell et al. provide the strongest geometric anchors for the present study by identifying representative favourable propeller heights near \(0.21c\) for the ideal no-nacelle case and near \(0.12c\) once nacelle effects are included.\cite{Hawkswell2022} Agrawal et al. justify the dependence of blown-section behaviour on \(\alpha\), blowing level, flap deflection, and jet-height ratio \(h/c\), while also noting that propulsor position and motor-axis angle affect the real flow beyond the simple theory.\cite{Agrawal2019} The present \(\beta\)-coefficients are therefore treated as paper-informed surrogate parameters to be calibrated, not as paper-derived constants.

These margins are converted to immersion factors through
\begin{equation}
\chi(\alpha)
=
\frac{1}{2}\left[1+\tanh\left(\frac{m}{\ell_\chi}\right)\right],
\end{equation}
where \(\ell_\chi = 0.02c\) is a smoothing length. The blown-strip contribution is then blended with the corresponding unblown section quantity. For example, the flap-strip lift contribution is formed as
\begin{equation}
\tilde{c}_{l,fb}^{\,\mathrm{raw}}
=
c_{l,fu}
+
\chi_f
\left[
\left(\frac{q_b}{q_\infty}\right)c_{l,fb}
-
c_{l,fu}
\right].
\end{equation}
This choice preserves the Cambridge interpretation that the jet-covered region behaves as a nominally uniform two-dimensional strip while also introducing the rapid loss of blown benefit that accompanies jet detachment from the wing.

To represent the second trend suggested by Agrawal et al., namely that sufficiently aggressive jet geometry can produce a sharper stall even before the wing has fully left the jet, the present code now applies a second bounded over-drop penalty to the blown-strip polar.\cite{Agrawal2019} With
\begin{equation}
h = \frac{\Delta z_p}{c},
\qquad
r_V = \frac{V_{eff}}{V_\infty},
\end{equation}
the present severity surrogate is
\begin{equation}
S_{od}
=
\frac{
\left[\max\left(0,\frac{h-h_{on}}{h_{ref}-h_{on}}\right)\right]^p
\left[\max(0,r_V-1)\right]^q
}{
1+
\left[\max\left(0,\frac{h-h_{on}}{h_{ref}-h_{on}}\right)\right]^p
\left[\max(0,r_V-1)\right]^q
}.
\end{equation}
The clean blown wing uses \(h_{on,c}=0.12\), while the slotted-flap branch uses \(h_{on,f}=0.16\); both branches use \(h_{ref}=0.21\), \(p=1.6\), and \(q=0.8\). These values are not literature-fitted constants. Rather, they are paper-informed initialization values chosen so that the clean blown wing becomes sensitive to excessive drop earlier than the slotted-flap case, in agreement with the qualitative trends of Refs.~\citenum{Hawkswell2022,Agrawal2019}. The penalty acts mainly near stall by shifting the apparent peak-lift location to lower \(\alpha\) and increasing the post-stall decay rate, while leaving the low-\(\alpha\) portion of the curve only weakly altered.

For the selected rank-6 concept, the current flap geometry produces
\begin{equation}
f_{cu} \approx 0.126,\quad
f_{fu} \approx 0.315,\quad
f_{cb} \approx 0.224,\quad
f_{fb} \approx 0.335.
\end{equation}
The actual operating point at the low-speed condition is
\begin{equation}
C_\mu \approx 1.54,\qquad
V_{\mathrm{eff}} = \SI{14.622}{m/s},
\end{equation}
not the upper bound \(C_\mu=2.5\) shown in the section comparison figure. Consequently, a blown section curve that approaches \(c_l \approx 9\) does not imply a whole-wing \(C_L \approx 9\). Only one-third of the wing is simultaneously blown and flapped in the present configuration, and the remaining two-thirds of the wing continue to operate at lower local lift levels.

This point is central to the interpretation of the present results. The section-response figure is the correct plot for comparing surrogate strip behavior against the literature. The whole-wing \(C_L\) plot is the correct plot for judging whether the selected configuration can meet the aircraft-level low-speed requirement.

\section{Results for the Selected Rank-6 Concept}

\subsection{Recommended flap and aileron geometry}

The current rectangular-wing sizing recommendation is:
\begin{itemize}
\item flap span fraction \(= 0.65\) of semispan,
\item flap chord fraction \(= 0.34\),
\item flap deflection \(= \SI{40}{deg}\),
\item aileron span fraction \(= 0.28\) of semispan,
\item aileron chord fraction \(= 0.28\).
\end{itemize}

The corresponding low-speed and control metrics are:
\begin{itemize}
\item common-\(\alpha\) freestream-referenced \(C_{L,\max} = 6.004\),
\item equivalent stall speed \(V_s = \SI{4.365}{m/s}\),
\item low-speed lift margin at \(\SI{4}{m/s}\): \(-16.0\%\),
\item estimated roll rate at \(\delta_a = \SI{14}{deg}\): \(\SI{30.9}{deg/s}\),
\item estimated roll rate at \(\delta_a = \SI{20}{deg}\): \(\SI{44.2}{deg/s}\),
\item prop-disk overlap over the flap: \(8/10\) disks, corresponding to \(80\%\) overlap of the positive-half prop count and \(60\%\) overlap of the positive-half disk span.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation}

Four observations follow directly from Figs.~\ref{fig:section_polars}--\ref{fig:low_speed_aileron}. First, the revised section model now produces the expected ordering: the flapped-and-blown wing curve lies above the blowing-only curve, but only by a modest amount. At the peak of the present whole-wing curve, the flap contributes roughly \(\Delta C_L \approx 0.16\) beyond the blowing-only case. Second, the whole-wing result remains well below the \(\SI{4}{m/s}\) requirement. The current corrected model therefore indicates that the selected rank-6 configuration is close to, but does not yet satisfy, the target low-speed condition. Third, the section figure is still useful, but only when interpreted as a strip-level diagnostic. Figure~\ref{fig:total_cl}, not Fig.~\ref{fig:section_polars}, is the appropriate figure for aircraft-level lift assessment. Fourth, the additional wing-only \(C_M\) and low-speed aileron proxies show that the present high-lift configuration carries a meaningful trim cost while still retaining nonzero roll authority in the blown low-speed regime.

At the present corrected optimum, the all-high-lift branch reaches \(C_{L,\max}\approx 6.00\) at \(\alpha \approx 13.3^\circ\). At that point the wing-only pitching moment is \(C_M \approx -0.90\), compared with \(C_M \approx -0.50\) for the blowing-only branch. The current low-speed aileron proxy indicates that approximately \(59\%\) of the aileron span lies inside the blown intervals, the local dynamic-pressure ratio on the aileron is about \(8.3\), and the resulting low-speed roll-rate proxy is about \(13\ \mathrm{deg/s}\) at the nominal \(14^\circ\) aileron deflection. These values should still be interpreted as wing-only concept metrics rather than full-aircraft handling-quality predictions.

\section{Motor-Height Trade Study}

\subsection{Trade formulation}

To isolate the effect of motor vertical position, the selected rank-6 propulsion architecture and the selected flap/aileron geometry are held fixed while the propeller vertical drop is swept. The present study uses the following nondimensional motor-drop values:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta z_p}{c} \in \{0.00, 0.04, 0.08, 0.12, 0.16, 0.20, 0.24\}.
\end{equation}
For the selected rank-6 geometry, this corresponds to
\begin{equation}
\frac{\Delta z_p}{D_p} \in \{0.00, 0.10, 0.20, 0.30, 0.40, 0.50, 0.60\}.
\end{equation}
The current script no longer translates motor height into performance through a generic bonus factor. Instead, the same Cambridge-style jet-immersion criterion used in the wing model is applied while sweeping this chord-referenced height.
This choice mirrors the methodology of Hawkswell et al., in which the decisive low-speed mechanism is whether the wing remains immersed in the jet.\cite{Hawkswell2022} The present trade differs from the full Cambridge configuration study in one important respect: nacelle drag is not included. The motor-height sweep reported here is therefore a wing-only high-lift sensitivity study, not a full-aircraft optimum.

Two scenario branches are carried through the trade. The first is a clean blown wing with no flap deployed. The second retains the selected single-slotted flap. This split is important because the current over-drop penalty is intentionally weaker for the slotted-flap branch than for the clean branch. The model therefore asks two separate questions: how much drop is required to achieve satisfactory jet immersion, and how far can the motor be lowered before the clean and flapped high-lift systems begin to show a sharper stall penalty?

\subsection{Trade results}

The resulting summary no longer reduces to a simple plateau. Instead, both scenario branches now exhibit the intended three-part trend: an initial rise as immersion improves, a peak or plateau once the wing is well inside the jet, and then a renewed deterioration once the over-drop penalty becomes active. In the present model:
\begin{itemize}
\item the clean blown wing reaches its best performance near \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.08\) to \(0.12\), with \(C_{L,\max} \approx 5.85\) and an equivalent stall speed near \SI{4.42}{m/s},
\item the slotted-flap blown wing reaches its best performance near \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.12\), with \(C_{L,\max} \approx 6.00\) and an equivalent stall speed near \SI{4.36}{m/s},
\item the clean blown wing begins to pay the over-drop penalty earlier: by \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.16\), its severity parameter has risen to about \(0.37\) and both \(C_{L,\max}\) and post-stall behavior have deteriorated visibly,
\item the slotted-flap branch remains largely penalty-free through \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.16\), but begins to degrade by \(\Delta z_p/c = 0.20\), at which point the over-drop severity exceeds \(0.5\),
\item neither branch closes the \(\SI{4}{m/s}\) aircraft-level requirement under the present assumptions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation and limitations}

The current trade should be interpreted as a first-order sensitivity study, not as a final geometric optimum. It provides three useful insights:
\begin{enumerate}
\item The code now has a structured way to compare motor height as a design variable.
\item The baseline prop drop remains reasonable under the present assumptions because it places the wing very near full jet immersion while remaining below the onset of the strongest over-drop penalty.
\item The clean blown wing is more sensitive to excessive drop than the slotted-flap branch, which is consistent with the physical expectation that the flap system can tolerate a somewhat deeper jet placement before the stall sharpens.
\item In the absence of nacelle drag, the present motor-height trade now exhibits a shallow interior optimum rather than a pure plateau.
\end{enumerate}

The largest remaining modeling gap is that motor height still does not alter the slipstream footprint, contraction, or swirl in a resolved flowfield sense, and the present over-drop penalty remains only qualitatively calibrated against the MIT trends rather than quantitatively fitted to a dedicated data set.\cite{Agrawal2019} A higher-fidelity follow-on study should therefore replace the present immersion-plus-penalty surrogate with a CFD-calibrated or experiment-calibrated relationship and, for full-aircraft optimization, add the nacelle-drag penalty that was central to the Cambridge configuration study.

\section{Representative Motor Targeting}

The present repository now also converts the downselected propeller operating point into a representative motor/ESC target sheet. This step should not be interpreted as a final procurement decision; rather, it is a bridge between the aerodynamic downselection and a future hardware-specific propulsion study.

For the selected rank-6 concept, the current Stage~1 operating point implies approximately:
\begin{itemize}
\item per-motor peak shaft power \(\approx \SI{11.6}{W}\),
\item required peak electrical power \(\approx \SI{17.1}{W}\),
\item required peak current \(\approx \SI{1.16}{A}\),
\item required Kv \(\approx \SI{771}{rpm/V}\),
\item recommended Kv band \(\approx \SIrange{655}{886}{rpm/V}\).
\end{itemize}

The present repository then compares that target against representative local motor and ESC class data. For the current dataset, the closest motor class is typified by a SunnySky A2212 \(1000\ \mathrm{Kv}\) motor and the closest ESC class by a BLHeli-S \(8\ \mathrm{A}\) unit. These examples are intentionally conservative because the local class dataset is coarse and skewed toward commonly available hobby hardware. They should therefore be interpreted as class matches, not as optimized part numbers.

\section{Conclusions}

The repository now contains a coherent path from coarse propulsion downselection to rectangular-wing high-lift sizing and, finally, to a first motor-height sensitivity study. The main technical conclusions are:
\begin{itemize}
\item The Stage~1 blown-wing architecture screen is mathematically well defined and suitable for concept downselection.
\item For the selected rank-6 propulsion concept, the present rectangular-wing study supports a \(65\%\)-semispan single-slotted flap at \(c_f/c=0.34\) and \(\delta_f=\SI{40}{deg}\), together with a \(28\%\)-semispan aileron at \(c_a/c=0.28\).
\item Section curves approaching \(c_l\approx 9\) do not imply whole-wing \(C_L\approx 9\); the aircraft-level result must be assembled from the blown and unblown area fractions at common \(\alpha\).
\item After the present high-lift corrections and overlap-aware flap placement are applied, the selected rank-6 concept reaches \(C_{L,\max}\approx 6.00\) and an equivalent stall speed of \(\SI{4.36}{m/s}\), leaving a modest shortfall relative to the \(\SI{4.0}{m/s}\) target.
\item The same wing-only study now returns additional trim and low-speed control information: at the peak high-lift point, the present flap-and-blowing configuration carries a wing-only pitching moment near \(C_M\approx -0.90\) while the low-speed aileron proxy remains near \(13\ \mathrm{deg/s}\) at nominal deflection.
\item The present motor-height trade now combines a Cambridge-style immersion criterion with a bounded over-drop stall penalty: lowering the motor first increases lift by improving jet coverage, but sufficiently large drop eventually sharpens the stall and reduces the benefit again. Because nacelle drag is not yet included, the current sweep should still be interpreted as a wing-only high-lift trend rather than as a full-aircraft optimum.
\item The repository now also converts the aerodynamic downselection into a representative motor/ESC target sheet. For the current rank-6 concept, the implied per-motor requirement is modest --- about \(17\ \mathrm{W}\), \(1.16\ \mathrm{A}\), and \(771\ \mathrm{rpm/V}\) --- which suggests that the aerodynamic concept can be carried into hardware downselection without demanding unusually large motors, although a live vendor-backed search remains future work.
\end{itemize}
